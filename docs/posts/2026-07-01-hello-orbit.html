Thinking is cool

I write this as my Generative Adversarial Network is being trained at work and I have time to kill. I used to always find ISRO cool, a bit too intimidating. Why did I find it so fascinating? I guess because there is a lot of unknown about it. Why does that matter to me? Because at my core, I like solving problems. Why? No idea!

I guess humans are probably hard coded with this instinct. Otherwise we wouldn't have won the evolutionary lottery. Think about it, if we actually liked problem solving, instead of just facing it when it came or running from it, only then would we have learned to anticipate, prepare, think of future outcomes and consequences, a future chain of actions.

How do you define liked, or an inclination toward something? I would define it as choosing to act on it over indifference, or over the opposite task.

If that is true, and the assumption is that humans are hard coded with problem solving, that has a few implications. One, we can teach kids early on to bring out these innate skills, by giving them more problem solving tasks. What makes the best one, I don't know yet. Two, we can teach people with latent skills to bring it out. Tougher, but achievable, they would just have to practice.

We would have to prove this conjecture first, and model it. To answer that, I would need to study probability and modeling problems better. It's basically a mix of probability and statistics, math modeling, computational modeling, choosing the right frameworks that speak to each other, and probably computer vision eventually. At the core, the aim is to minimize the friction of the barrier to entry to something like squash, because ‘just go and practice’ is arbitrary, and I want to quantify it. I want to lower the barrier to entry of understanding these concepts.

If we are able to model what makes something easier and more user friendly, something people want to read or pick over anything else in the morning, we could basically bring out all these latent problem solving skills and collaborate across disciplines.

Of course it's not that simple. Learning itself is imbibed with a curve, repetition, and difficulty. What makes something captivating, a concept, is a difficult, multifaceted, multivariable question. My base case would be to stick to my domain, CS, math, squash.

Digressing Note lol : Take squash for example. Can we teach people to play better squash with math?

I guess I would be more attracted to something if it had to do with the physical world, something dynamic, something I can see around me over reading a text. That's just a personal preference, I think. That's where multimodal MLLMs come in. They could lower the friction, for example, displaying 3D things, or making the difference between numbers visible, like 1 million versus 1 billion.

Basically anything humans are currently limited in how they perceive. I am unsure if there is a study on how humans learn better with visualization. Components of a computer, a live simulation you can touch, play with, feel, how it’s all voltages. Maybe also the evolution of a system after x years. What does adding a parameter even do?

Apart from learning, multimodal AI could be really beneficial for devs too. I could tinker with a loss function and it would predict how my loss would evolve, and I could visualize which param or variable is giving a bad loss, maybe it's noise. Though I think noise tells a lot about a system, that's a separate train of thought.

When building large scale systems, it's really difficult to see where everything goes. In ML and AI especially, there are some things that can only be done with intuition, guesswork, brute force trial and error. This becomes recursive, in the sense that to build such a system we would need human intuition and guesswork, but it's only a one time build, and if done right it can do a lot, and the people whose skills are unearthed with this platform can even help to refine it. Basically in my head I'm thinking of designing a platform for devs, specifically AI/ML, that helps them with the tinkering. But I'm digressing, back to the MLLM.

There was new research about a model built with taste as its vector embedding. I would like to see something similar, multimodal, for smell. There can even be smell LLM to teach baking! With the rise of AI, people are scared of losing their jobs, or already are. But jobs solve problems, and as long as there are problems in the world, we will always need people on it.

And if we run out of problems on earth, the cosmos awaits us.
